# Store Comparison Reference

How to evaluate grocery stores by ingredient category, build a store-strength map for your area, and optimize shopping across multiple retailers.

---

## STORE EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

Not all stores are equally good for all ingredients. The goal is to identify which store is cheapest for each category and optimize shopping accordingly.

### The Three Store Types

**1. One-Stop Convenience (Walmart, Target)**
- Pros: Single trip, comparable pricing, private label strong
- Cons: Produce quality varies; specialty items limited
- Best for: Proteins, pantry staples, bulk items
- Avoid: Specialty/organic produce, premium items

**2. Premium/Quality (Whole Foods, Trader Joe's)**
- Pros: Better produce quality, pre-made meals, consistent pricing
- Cons: Higher prices, smaller portions, limited bulk options
- Best for: Organic produce, quality meats, ready-made components
- Avoid: Bulk buying, pantry staples

**3. Bulk Warehouse (Costco, Sam's Club)**
- Pros: Lowest per-unit cost, high quality
- Cons: Membership fees, large portions, storage required
- Best for: Proteins, pantry staples, freezer-friendly items
- Avoid: Produce (spoils before consumption), specialty items

```

  - Con: Higher prices on basics (pasta, oils)

```

```

- Buy produce at Trader Joe's (quality, consistent pricing)
- Buy pantry staples at Costco if bulk is viable; otherwise Walmart
- Buy frozen vegetables at Trader Joe's (saves prep time)
```

## COST-PER-ITEM COMPARISON LOGIC

When comparing prices across stores, standardize by unit to account for package size differences.

### Convert to Cost Per Unit

**Formula:** Total Price ÷ Item Quantity = Cost Per Unit

**Example: Eggs**
```
Walmart: $2.49 for 18 eggs = $0.138 per egg
Trader Joe's: $3.49 for 18 eggs = $0.194 per egg
Costco: $5.99 for 30 eggs = $0.200 per egg

Winner: Walmart at $0.138/egg
```

**Example: Chicken Thighs**
```
Walmart: $8.97 for 3 lbs = $2.99/lb
Trader Joe's: $10.47 for 3 lbs = $3.49/lb
Costco: $9.95 for 5 lbs = $1.99/lb

Winner: Costco at $1.99/lb
But: Costco minimum is 5 lbs. If household uses only 3 lbs/week, 2 lbs freeze and costs $0.10 extra per lb in freezer time. Net: Costco still saves $2/week ($104/year).
```

### Account for Storage & Spoilage

When a bulk purchase is cheaper per unit but requires freezing or risks spoilage:

```
Cost advantage: Costco chicken is $1/lb cheaper than Walmart
Household use: 3 lbs/week
Minimum Costco purchase: 5 lbs

Cost analysis:
- Walmart option: 3 lbs @ $2.99 = $8.97
- Costco option: 5 lbs @ $1.99 = $9.95 (freeze 2 lbs)
- Freezer cost: Assume 2 lbs cost $0.10/lb extra for freezer time = $0.20
- Net Costco cost: $9.95 + $0.20 = $10.15

Wait, that's MORE expensive. But it resets if the household actually uses 5 lbs/week.
```

**Decision rule:** Only recommend bulk if the household can actually consume it.

## MULTI-STORE SHOPPING STRATEGY

Once you've identified store strengths, optimize shopping routes.

### Strategy 1: One-Stop Shopping (Convenience)
- Shop at one store for all items, even if not cheapest
- Time savings: 1 trip (30 min) vs. 2–3 trips (90 min)
- Cost premium: Usually 5–10% over optimal
- Best for: Busy households, small budgets where time is valuable

**Example:**
"You could save $3 by buying chicken at Costco instead of Walmart, but it requires a separate trip. At your hourly rate, that's not worth it. Stick with Walmart for everything."

### Strategy 2: Two-Store Split
- Buy proteins at Store A, produce at Store B
- Time cost: 2 trips (60 min)
- Savings: Usually 10–15% vs. one-stop

**Example:**
"Buy eggs and chicken at Walmart (cheapest). Buy produce at Trader Joe's (quality + frozen veg). Two stops, $8 savings/week."

### Strategy 3: Costco + Fill-In Store
- Buy bulk proteins and pantry at Costco (monthly or biweekly)
- Fill in produce and dairy at a local store weekly
- Time cost: 1 Costco trip (1x/month) + 1 local trip (weekly)
- Savings: Usually 15–20% vs. one-stop

**Example:**
"Buy 10 lbs chicken and 2 bottles olive oil at Costco monthly. Buy produce and eggs weekly at Walmart. This maximizes savings while keeping local trips to once/week."

## STORE SELECTION CONSTRAINTS

Always ask the user about practical constraints before recommending a store strategy.

### Membership Fees
- Costco: $65/year ($1.25/week)
- Sam's Club: $45/year ($0.85/week)
- Only worth it if you save >$2/week through bulk buying

### Drive Time
- If Costco is 20 min away vs. Walmart 5 min away, time cost matters
- Costco trip: 20 min drive + 30 min shopping = 50 min
- Is $10 savings worth 50 minutes of your time?

### Household Size & Appetite
- Costco bulk portions work for family of 4+
- Smaller households (2–3 people) may waste more through spoilage

### Freezer Space
- Bulk chicken requires freezer room
- Ask: "Do you have freezer space for 5 lbs of chicken?"

### Produce Quality
- Some stores have better produce than others (subjective)
- Trader Joe's is known for quality; Walmart for budget
- User preference may override price optimization

## ADJUSTING STORE STRATEGY IF CONSTRAINTS CHANGE

If the user's situation changes, revisit the store map.

### Scenario: "Costco is too expensive"
- Maybe Costco prices have risen, or user's household size changed
- Workflow: Pull new prices, recalculate cost per unit, identify new winner
- Don't defend Costco; pivot to the data

### Scenario: "I don't have time for multiple stores"
- Workflow: Switch to one-stop strategy, even if it costs 5–10% more
- Show the trade-off: "Walmart is 8% more expensive but saves you 60 min/week. That's worth it."

### Scenario: "We moved to a new area"
- Workflow: Ask about available stores, rebuild the store-strength map for the new location
- Prices vary by region; don't reuse old data

---

## STORE STRATEGY SUCCESS CRITERIA

A store strategy works if:

✅ Saves 10%+ compared to shopping at one store everywhere
✅ Requires <2 shopping trips per week
✅ Accounts for actual household appetite and storage
✅ Factors in time cost (membership fees, drive time)
✅ User actually sticks to it (if it's too complex, they won't follow it)

If the optimal strategy requires 3 stores and the user only does 1-stop, it's not successful. Simplicity matters.
